Your Mental Health is Your Health

Breaking the Stigma

Breaking the stigma around mental health starts with open, honest conversations. Too often, people feel ashamed to seek help due to misconceptions or fear of judgment. But mental health is just as important as physical health—it affects how we think, feel, and live. By talking openly, we can challenge stereotypes, promote understanding, and show that seeking support is a sign of strength, not weakness. Everyone deserves compassion and care, regardless of their struggles. Together, we can create a society where no one feels alone or silenced. Let’s break the stigma and prioritize mental well-being for all. It starts with us.

Statistics

  • 1 in 5 adults in the U.S. experience a mental health condition each year.
  • 50% of mental health conditions begin by age 14, and 75% develop by age 24.
  • Depression is the leading cause of disability worldwide, affecting over 280 million people.
  • Suicide is the second leading cause of death among individuals aged 15-29 years.
  • Over 700,000 people die by suicide annually, with many more attempting it.
  • Only 43% of people with mental health conditions in the U.S. receive professional care.
  • Workplace stress costs the global economy over $1 trillion annually in lost productivity.

How mental affects physical

Mental health and physical health are deeply connected. Chronic stress, anxiety, and depression can weaken the immune system, increase inflammation, and elevate the risk of heart disease, high blood pressure, and diabetes. Poor mental health can also lead to unhealthy habits like overeating, smoking, or lack of exercise, further impacting physical well-being. Conversely, physical conditions like chronic pain or illness can worsen mental health, leading to feelings of hopelessness or anxiety. Taking care of your mental health improves overall well-being, energy, and resilience. Prioritizing both mind and body creates a healthier, more balanced life. They’re equally important to thrive.
